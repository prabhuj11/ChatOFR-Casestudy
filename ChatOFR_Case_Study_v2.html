02 · Discovery

Seeing the Opportunity
Before Anyone Else Did

OFR was deploying an internal chatbot using Claude Sonnet and Haiku to help staff navigate SharePoint sites across Treasury. It was a purely internal tool. I saw a different application.

The Pitch
"What if we adapt this chatbot architecture to automate the JADE researcher onboarding workflow? Instead of asking researchers to navigate ServiceNow and Collibra manually, the AI handles the routing, validation, and provisioning — automatically."
Why it was feasible
  • ✓ Claude Sonnet already deployed inside Treasury
  • ✓ ServiceNow and Collibra both had APIs
  • ✓ Onboarding workflows were rule-based and deterministic
Why it needed careful handling
  • △ Federal regulatory environment — failure is visible and costly
  • △ Five agencies, five different entitlement models
  • △ AI error in access provisioning carries regulatory risk
03 · Human Impact

Behind Every Request:
A Researcher Whose Work Waited

ChatOFR was not about automation for its own sake. It was about what 33 recovered days meant for the people doing the research — and for the systemic risk oversight that depended on them.

Before ChatOFR
45 days

A researcher arriving from the Federal Reserve Board to work on systemic risk analysis would spend six weeks navigating tickets and waiting for manual reviews — before writing a single line of analysis code. Research that informs financial policy decisions waited because of a broken process.

After ChatOFR
12 days

The same researcher now interacts with a conversational AI that validates their credentials, routes their request, and provisions their dataset access — automatically, with a human in the loop only for edge cases. 33 days returned to research that matters.

Accessibility & Equity Design

Researchers across five agencies brought different technical comfort levels. An economist at the SEC has different needs than a data scientist at FRB. I prioritized plain language in all AI responses, clear escalation paths for researchers who preferred human support, and consistent behavior across agency contexts — so no user was disadvantaged by their technical background or institutional familiarity with the platform.

09 · The Challenge

Leadership Wanted to Launch Now.
I Disagreed.

After the ServiceNow integration was complete, leadership saw working technology and wanted an immediate big-bang rollout to all five agencies. I pushed back and made the case for the phased approach.

Leadership's Position
"We have working technology. Let's launch to all agencies now."
The ServiceNow integration worked. Leadership wanted to show impact quickly. Waiting felt like unnecessary delay when the product was functional and ready.
My Position
"We've proven one layer. We haven't proven the product."
ServiceNow was working but Collibra wasn't integrated yet. We had no external user validation. In a federal regulatory environment, a failed rollout damages trust in AI across the entire ecosystem — trust that takes years to rebuild.
✓

Leadership agreed — immediately

I made a reasoned case: the fastest path to full adoption in a trust-sensitive environment is often a slower, more deliberate one. The FDIC pilot discovered issues we hadn't anticipated. We fixed them before anyone else saw them. The full rollout was smoother because of the pilot, not despite it. Leadership's willingness to hear a contrary view and change course is exactly the culture that lets products succeed.

"In a federal regulatory environment, a failed public rollout doesn't just set back one product — it sets back trust in AI across the entire agency ecosystem. The nested pilot approach meant every layer was proven before the next one was added."

11 · Post-Launch Reflection

What I'd Do Differently.
What I'd Keep.

The product shipped and achieved its goals. Honest post-launch reflection reveals what I'd change — and what principles I'd carry forward into every AI product I build.

△ What I'd do differently

  • Involve legal and compliance earlier in the intent classification design — we discovered edge cases during the FDIC pilot that a compliance review would have caught sooner.
  • Build the audit trail and logging framework before writing the first workflow. Governance is a design constraint, not an afterthought.
  • Define the exception handling model in writing before engineering started building. When the AI escalates to a human needed more precision earlier.
  • Test with researchers of lower technical comfort levels earlier in the pilot. We over-indexed on technically sophisticated FDIC users and discovered additional needs only after full rollout.

✓ What I'd keep

  • The nested pilot strategy. Every layer proven before the next one depended on it. Non-negotiable for AI products in regulated environments.
  • Recruiting a pilot agency who would give honest feedback, not just compliance. FDIC's candor shaped the product meaningfully.
  • Treating FDIC as a design partner during the pilot — not a test subject. Their feedback shaped what got built.
  • Starting with one workflow, not all of them. The instinct to prove before you scale is right every time.
"Government clients need to trust before they evangelize. The fastest path to full adoption is often a slower, more deliberate one."
